# Tax Optimization

Tax-advantaged strategies for US and Canadian investors.

## CRITICAL: 2026 TCJA Expiration

Unless Congress acts, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions expire after 12/31/2025:
- **Tax brackets revert** to pre-2018 rates (top rate: 37% → 39.6%)
- **Standard deduction roughly halves** (~$15,000 single → ~$8,000)
- **Personal exemption returns** (~$5,300 per person)
- **SALT deduction cap ($10,000) removed**
- **Estate tax exemption drops** to ~$7 million (from ~$14M)
- **Child Tax Credit drops** to $1,000 (from $2,000)
- **QBI deduction (Section 199A) expires**
- **Action items:** Accelerate Roth conversions in 2025, consider income timing, review estate plans

This is the single most impactful US financial planning event for 2025-2026.

## US Tax Optimization

### Retirement Account Strategies

**401(k)**
- Contribution limit: $23,500 (2025), +$7,500 catch-up if 50+, +$11,250 super catch-up if age 60-63 (SECURE 2.0)
- Traditional 401(k): pre-tax contributions, taxed on withdrawal
- Roth 401(k): after-tax contributions, tax-free withdrawals
- Employer match: contribute at least enough to get full match (guaranteed 50-100% return)
- Rule of thumb: Traditional if current tax rate > expected retirement rate; Roth if lower now
- **SECURE 2.0 updates:** Auto-enrollment required for new plans (3-10%, auto-escalate to 10%), student loan matching, emergency savings accounts ($2,500), Roth employer match now available

**Traditional IRA**
- Contribution limit: $7,000 (2025), +$1,000 catch-up if 50+
- Fully deductible if no workplace plan, or if AGI below threshold with workplace plan
- 2025 thresholds: $79,000 single / $126,000 married (for full deduction with workplace plan)

**Roth IRA**
- Same $7,000 limit
- Income limits: $150,000-$165,000 single / $236,000-$246,000 married for full contribution (2025)
- Tax-free growth and qualified withdrawals (5-year rule + age 59.5)
- No RMDs during owner's lifetime
- Best for: young investors, those expecting higher future tax rates

**Backdoor Roth IRA**
- For high earners above Roth income limits
- Contribute to non-deductible Traditional IRA -> convert to Roth
- Pro-rata rule: if you have pre-tax IRA balances, conversion is partially taxable
- Solution: roll pre-tax IRA into 401(k) before converting

**Mega Backdoor Roth**
- After-tax contributions to 401(k) above $23,500, up to $70,000 total limit (2025, $77,500 if 50+)
- Allows up to ~$46,500 additional Roth savings after employee contributions
- Requires plan to allow after-tax contributions + in-service distributions or in-plan Roth conversion
- Convert after-tax contributions to Roth (minimal tax if done quickly)
- Not all employers offer this — common at large tech companies and financial firms
- **Status (2025):** Still allowed. Build Back Better proposed eliminating it but was never enacted. SECURE 2.0 did not address it.

**Roth Conversion Ladder**
- Convert Traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth in low-income years
- Each conversion has a 5-year waiting period before penalty-free withdrawal
- Key FIRE strategy: convert during early retirement when income is low
- Fill up lower tax brackets strategically

### HSA Triple Tax Advantage
- **Tax-deductible contributions:** $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025), +$1,000 catch-up if 55+
- **Tax-free growth:** invest HSA funds like a retirement account
- **Tax-free withdrawals:** for qualified medical expenses (at any time, even years later)
- After age 65: withdrawals for any purpose taxed as ordinary income (like Traditional IRA)
- **Stealth retirement account strategy:** pay medical costs out of pocket, let HSA compound, withdraw tax-free later with saved receipts
- Requires high-deductible health plan (HDHP)

### Capital Gains Optimization
- **Long-term (held >1 year):** 0% (up to ~$47K single), 15% (up to ~$518K), 20% (above)
- **Short-term (held <=1 year):** taxed as ordinary income
- **Net Investment Income Tax:** additional 3.8% on investment income above $200K single / $250K married
- Strategy: hold investments >1 year when possible

**Tax-Loss Harvesting**
- Sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains
- Losses offset gains dollar-for-dollar; excess losses deduct up to $3,000/year from ordinary income
- Remaining losses carry forward indefinitely
- **Wash sale rule:** cannot buy "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after sale
- Workaround: buy similar but not identical fund (e.g., sell VTI, buy ITOT)

**Tax Gain Harvesting (0% Bracket Exploitation)**
- Sell appreciated investments when taxable income is in the **0% long-term capital gains bracket**
- Immediately repurchase the same investment (no wash sale rule on gains — only applies to losses)
- Resets cost basis higher, permanently eliminating future tax on those gains
- **2025 US 0% LTCG thresholds:** Up to ~$48,350 single / ~$96,700 MFJ taxable income
- **Who benefits most:** Early retirees, gap year between jobs, part-time workers, retirees before SS/RMDs
- **Example:** Married couple, $40K income, $30K standard deduction = $10K taxable. Room for ~$86K of gains at 0% tax. Saving: $12,900+ in future taxes (15% × $86K).
- **Watch for:** ACA premium subsidy cliffs (gains increase MAGI), state taxes (some states don't conform to 0% federal rate), NIIT above $250K MFJ

**Cryptocurrency Tax Treatment (US)**
- Crypto is treated as **property** — every sale, trade, or exchange is a taxable event
- Long-term rates apply if held >1 year; short-term (ordinary income) if ≤1 year
- Mining/staking income taxed as ordinary income at fair market value when received
- Losses can offset gains; $3,000/year against ordinary income (same as stock losses)
- **No wash sale rule for crypto** (as of 2025) — can sell at loss and immediately rebuy
- Form 8949 required for reporting; exchanges issue 1099 forms
- **Spot Bitcoin ETFs** (IBIT, FBTC, approved Jan 2024): taxed like any ETF — simpler than direct crypto holding

**Asset Location**
- Bonds, REITs in tax-deferred accounts (high-tax distributions)
- High-growth stocks in Roth accounts (tax-free growth)
- US index funds in taxable accounts (tax-efficient, qualified dividends, potential step-up basis at death)

### Self-Employment Tax Optimization
- **QBI Deduction:** 20% deduction on qualified business income (Section 199A), phases out above $191,950 single / $383,900 married (2025)
- **SEP IRA:** Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $69,000 (2025)
- **Solo 401(k):** Employee contribution ($23,500) + employer (25% of income), same $69,000 cap
- **S-Corp election:** Pay yourself "reasonable salary" + take remainder as distributions (avoid self-employment tax on distributions). Generally beneficial when net income > ~$40K-$60K.
- **Home office deduction:** Simplified method $5/sq ft (max $1,500) or actual expenses pro-rated by office area
- **Vehicle:** Standard mileage $0.70/mile (2025) or actual expenses

### 529 Plans
- State tax deduction in many states (check your state)
- Tax-free growth and withdrawals for qualified education expenses
- No federal annual limit; gift tax exclusion: $19,000/recipient (2025)
- Superfunding: contribute 5 years of gift exclusion at once ($95,000 in 2025)
- Unused funds: can roll up to $35,000 lifetime to beneficiary's Roth IRA (SECURE 2.0) — account must be open 15+ years, subject to annual Roth contribution limits
- Lifetime contribution limit: $235,000-$550,000 depending on state

## Canada Tax Optimization

### RRSP vs TFSA Decision Framework

| Factor | RRSP Better | TFSA Better |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Current marginal rate | High (>30%) | Low (<30%) |
| Expected retirement rate | Lower than now | Same or higher than now |
| Income stability | Stable high income | Variable or growing income |
| Need for flexibility | No (locked in) | Yes (withdraw anytime) |
| HBP/LLP eligibility | Planning to use | Not applicable |
| Age | Closer to peak earning years | Young, early career |

**General rule:** If your marginal tax rate now > expected rate in retirement, RRSP wins. Otherwise, TFSA.

**RRSP**
- Contribution room: 18% of prior year earned income, up to $32,490 (2025), $33,610 (2026)
- Unused room carries forward indefinitely
- Contributions reduce taxable income (immediate tax refund)
- Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
- Must convert to RRIF by end of year turning 71
- **Pro tip:** Invest the tax refund from RRSP contributions (don't spend it)

**TFSA**
- Contribution room: $7,000/year (2025-2026), cumulative since 2009 or age 18
- **Cumulative room:** $95,000 (2025), $102,000 (2026) for someone eligible since 2009
- Historical limits: $5,000 (2009-2012), $5,500 (2013-2014), $10,000 (2015), $5,500 (2016-2018), $6,000 (2019-2022), $6,500 (2023), $7,000 (2024-2026)
- No tax deduction on contributions
- Growth and withdrawals completely tax-free
- Withdrawn amounts re-added to contribution room the following year
- No age limit, no conversion required
- Most flexible account in Canada

### FHSA (First Home Savings Account)
- $8,000/year, $40,000 lifetime limit
- Tax-deductible contributions (like RRSP) + tax-free withdrawals for qualifying home purchase (like TFSA)
- **Best of both worlds** - use before RRSP HBP
- Can carry forward up to $8,000 of unused room (max $16,000 in a single catch-up year)
- Can combine with RRSP Home Buyers' Plan ($60,000) for total $100,000 tax-advantaged first home savings
- Must be first-time buyer (haven't owned home in current year or previous 4 calendar years)
- Account must be closed by 15th anniversary of opening or December 31 of year turning 71
- **Strategy:** Even if unsure about buying, open to start the 15-year clock; can transfer to RRSP later without using RRSP room
- Can contribute now and defer the deduction to a higher-income year (like RRSP)

### Dividend Tax Credit
- Canadian eligible dividends: grossed up by 38%, then federal + provincial tax credit applied
- **Effective tax rate on eligible dividends is lower than interest income or employment income**
- In lower tax brackets, eligible dividends can have negative effective tax rate
- **Strategy:** Hold Canadian dividend-paying stocks in non-registered accounts (not in RRSP/TFSA where the credit is wasted)
- Foreign dividends: no tax credit, taxed as regular income

### Capital Gains
- **Inclusion rate:** 50% on first $250,000 of gains per year, 66.7% on gains above $250,000 (individuals). Corporations/trusts: 66.7% on all gains.
- Only the included portion is added to taxable income
- **Superficial loss rule:** Cannot claim loss if you buy back same or identical property within 30 days (before or after sale)
- **Principal residence exemption:** No capital gains tax on primary home (designation required on sale)
- **Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption:** $1,250,000 (2025, up from $1,016,836) on qualifying small business corporation shares or farm/fishing property
- **Canadian Entrepreneurs' Incentive:** Phasing in — reduces inclusion rate to 33.33% on up to $2M lifetime of eligible gains

### Incorporation for High Earners
- **Small business deduction:** 9% federal tax on first $500,000 of active business income
- Combined federal/provincial rate typically 11-12.5% (vs personal rates of 45-54%)
- **Retain earnings in corporation** to defer personal tax
- **Income splitting:** Pay family members reasonable salary, issue dividends
- **Passive income threshold:** More than $50,000 passive income reduces small business deduction
- **Professional corporations:** Available to doctors, lawyers, accountants, engineers (rules vary by province)
- **When to incorporate:** Generally when business net income consistently exceeds $50,000-$80,000 AND you don't need all the income personally

### RESP (Registered Education Savings Plan)
- **CESG:** Government matches 20% of first $2,500 contributed per year = $500/year, $7,200 lifetime per beneficiary
- Additional CESG for lower-income families (up to 40% on first $500, income under ~$55,867)
- **CLB (Canada Learning Bond):** Up to $2,000 for eligible low-income families ($500 initial + $100/year)
- No annual contribution limit, $50,000 lifetime per beneficiary
- Growth is tax-deferred, taxed in student's hands on withdrawal (usually minimal tax)
- **Provincial grants:** BC Training and Education Savings Grant ($1,200), Quebec QESI (10-20% match)

## Estate and Gift Planning

### US
- **Federal estate tax exemption:** $13,990,000 per person (2025), ~$27.98M per couple. **Drops to ~$7M in 2026 if TCJA expires.**
- **Annual gift exclusion:** $19,000 per recipient per year (2025), no tax or reporting needed
- **Step-up in basis at death:** Inherited assets get cost basis reset to date-of-death value (eliminates capital gains)
- **Beneficiary designations:** Retirement accounts and life insurance pass outside of probate

### Canada
- **No estate tax** but deemed disposition at death triggers capital gains on non-registered assets
- **No gift tax** but attribution rules apply (income earned on gifts to spouse or minor children attributed back to giver)
- **Beneficiary designations:** RRSP/RRIF/TFSA can name beneficiary or successor holder (avoids probate)
- **Probate fees:** Vary by province (highest in Ontario and BC)
- **Principal residence exemption:** Applies on death for qualifying home
