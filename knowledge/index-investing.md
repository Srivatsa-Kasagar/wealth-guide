# Index Investing

Passive index investing methodology for long-term wealth building.

## Philosophy

Index investing is built on the premise that consistently beating the market after fees is extremely difficult. By owning the entire market at minimal cost, investors capture market returns while avoiding the risks of stock picking and market timing.

**Core principles:**
- Markets are broadly efficient - most active managers underperform their benchmark after fees
- Costs matter enormously over time (1% annual fee can reduce final portfolio by 25%+ over 30 years)
- Diversification is the only free lunch in investing
- Time in market beats timing the market

**Historical returns:** US stock market has averaged ~10% nominal / ~7% real (inflation-adjusted) annually over the past century. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results, but the long-term trend of productive economies is upward.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of market conditions.

**How it works:**
- Invest the same amount every month (e.g., $500)
- Buy more shares when prices are low, fewer when high
- Reduces impact of short-term volatility
- Removes emotion from investment decisions

**Lump sum vs DCA:** Statistically, lump sum investing outperforms DCA ~67% of the time (because markets trend upward). But DCA is psychologically easier and reduces regret risk. For regular paychecks, DCA is the natural approach.

**Automation:** Set up automatic investments on payday. Most brokerages support automatic purchases of index funds/ETFs.

## Asset Allocation Models

### Age-Based Rules
- **110 minus age** = stock allocation percentage (e.g., age 30 → 80% stocks)
- More aggressive: 120 minus age
- More conservative: 100 minus age
- Adjust based on risk tolerance, job stability, and other income sources

### Risk-Based Models

| Risk Profile | Stocks | Bonds | Expected Return | Max Drawdown |
|-------------|--------|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| Conservative | 30-40% | 60-70% | 4-6% | -15 to -20% |
| Moderate | 50-70% | 30-50% | 5-8% | -25 to -35% |
| Aggressive | 80-100% | 0-20% | 7-10% | -40 to -55% |

### Three-Fund Portfolio
The simplest diversified portfolio using only 3 funds:
1. Domestic total stock market
2. International total stock market
3. Total bond market

Ratio depends on risk tolerance and age.

### Tax-Advantaged Account Priority
1. **401(k) up to employer match** - guaranteed 50-100% return
2. **Roth IRA** - $7,000/year, tax-free growth forever
3. **Remaining 401(k)** - up to $23,500/year total
4. **HSA** - $4,300/$8,550/year, triple tax advantage (if eligible)
5. **Taxable brokerage** - no limits, most flexible

### Target-Date Funds
- Vanguard Target Retirement 20XX, Fidelity Freedom 20XX
- Single fund that auto-adjusts stock/bond mix as you approach retirement
- Slightly higher expense ratio (~0.12-0.15%) but completely hands-off
- Best for: people who want zero maintenance

### Tax-Efficient Fund Placement
- **Tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA):** bonds, REITs (high-tax distributions)
- **Roth IRA/Roth 401k:** highest growth potential (stocks, small-cap) - tax-free growth
- **Taxable accounts:** US stock index funds (low turnover, qualified dividends), tax-managed funds

### Canadian Couch Potato Portfolios
Popularized by Dan Bortolotti. Model portfolios using low-cost ETFs:
- **Conservative:** VBAL (60/40)
- **Balanced:** VGRO (80/20)
- **Aggressive:** VEQT (100% equity)

### Foreign Withholding Tax Considerations
- US-listed ETFs (VTI, VXUS) in RRSP: 15% US withholding tax exempt (tax treaty)
- US-listed ETFs in TFSA: 15% US withholding tax APPLIES (no treaty exemption)
- Canadian-listed ETFs (VEQT, XEQT): withholding tax embedded in MER, simpler
- **Bottom line:** For simplicity, use Canadian-listed all-in-one ETFs. The tax drag difference is small for most investors.

## Rebalancing

### When to Rebalance
- **Calendar-based:** Once per year (simple, effective)
- **Threshold-based:** When any asset class drifts >5% from target (e.g., 60% target stocks, rebalance if >65% or <55%)
- **Contribution-based:** Direct new contributions to underweight assets

### How to Rebalance
1. Check current allocation vs target
2. Redirect new contributions to underweight assets (tax-free rebalancing)
3. If needed, sell overweight assets in tax-advantaged accounts first (no tax consequences)
4. In taxable accounts: consider tax implications before selling

## Common Mistakes
- **Market timing:** Waiting for a dip to invest (miss gains while waiting)
- **Performance chasing:** Buying last year's winners (past performance ≠ future results)
- **Over-trading:** Frequent buying/selling increases costs and taxes
- **Home country bias:** Over-allocating to domestic stocks (US = 60% of global market, Canada = 3%)
- **Checking too often:** Daily portfolio checks increase anxiety and bad decisions
- **Abandoning strategy during downturns:** The plan works if you stick with it
