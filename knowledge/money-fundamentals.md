# Money Fundamentals

Budgeting, emergency funds, debt management, credit, insurance, and behavioral finance.

## Budgeting Frameworks

### 50/30/20 Rule (Elizabeth Warren)
- **50% Needs:** Housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments, transportation
- **30% Wants:** Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, hobbies, travel
- **20% Savings/Debt:** Emergency fund, investments, extra debt payments
- Simple starting point; adjust percentages based on income and goals
- High cost-of-living areas may need 60/20/20 or 70/15/15

### Zero-Based Budgeting (Dave Ramsey)
- Every dollar gets assigned a job before the month begins
- Income minus all expenses and savings = $0
- Forces intentional decisions about every dollar
- More work but more control

### Pay Yourself First (Reverse Budgeting)
- Automate savings/investing immediately after payday
- Spend whatever's left
- Simple: set up automatic transfers, live on the rest
- Best for people who hate tracking expenses

### Envelope System
- Allocate cash to spending categories (physical or digital envelopes)
- When envelope is empty, stop spending in that category
- Digital versions: YNAB (You Need A Budget), Goodbudget
- Effective for variable spending categories (dining, entertainment)

### Tools
- YNAB ($99/year): zero-based, powerful but learning curve
- Monarch Money ($99/year): automatic tracking, clean interface
- Spreadsheet: free, full control, Google Sheets budget templates
- Bank apps: many now have built-in budgeting features

## Emergency Fund

### Why You Need One
- Job loss (average job search: 3-6 months)
- Medical emergencies
- Car/home repairs
- Income interruption (freelancers, commission-based)
- Prevents going into debt for unexpected expenses

### How Much
- **Minimum starter:** $1,000 (while paying off high-interest debt)
- **Standard:** 3-6 months of essential expenses
- **3 months:** Dual income household, stable employment, strong job market
- **6 months:** Single income, variable income, specialized career, self-employed
- **12 months:** Very risk-averse, approaching retirement, volatile industry

### Where to Keep It
- **US:** High-yield savings account (HYSA) - Ally, Marcus, Discover (~4-5% APY)
- **Canada:** High-interest savings account (HISA) - EQ Bank, Tangerine, or GIC ladder (~4-5%)
- Money market funds as alternative
- NOT in stocks, crypto, or anything volatile
- Must be accessible within 1-3 business days

### How to Build It
1. Open a separate savings account (out of sight, out of mind)
2. Set automatic transfer on payday (even $50/month)
3. Build to $1,000 first, then scale to 3-6 months
4. Redirect debt payments to emergency fund once high-interest debt is paid
5. Treat it as non-negotiable (not for vacations or wants)

## Debt Payoff Strategies

### Avalanche Method (Mathematically Optimal)
1. List debts by interest rate (highest to lowest)
2. Make minimum payments on all debts
3. Put all extra money toward highest-rate debt
4. When paid off, roll payment to next highest rate
- **Saves the most money** in total interest

### Snowball Method (Psychologically Effective)
1. List debts by balance (smallest to largest)
2. Make minimum payments on all debts
3. Put all extra money toward smallest balance
4. When paid off, roll payment to next smallest
- **Builds momentum** through quick wins (Dave Ramsey's recommendation)

### When to Use Which
- **Avalanche:** If you're disciplined and motivated by math
- **Snowball:** If you need motivation from quick wins
- **Hybrid:** Kill any 20%+ APR debt first, then snowball the rest

### Debt Consolidation
- Combine multiple debts into one lower-rate loan
- Balance transfer cards: 0% intro APR for 12-21 months (watch 3-5% transfer fee)
- Personal loans: fixed rate, fixed term
- Only consolidate if rate is lower AND you stop adding new debt

### US-Specific
- **Student loan forgiveness:** PSLF (10 years public service), IBR/SAVE (income-driven, forgiveness after 20-25 years)
- **Student loan interest deduction:** Up to $2,500/year
- **Bankruptcy:** Chapter 7 (liquidation) or Chapter 13 (repayment plan)

### Canada-Specific
- **Consumer proposal:** Negotiate to pay less than full amount, affects credit 3 years post-completion
- **Orderly Payment of Debts:** Court-ordered 5% interest (Alberta only)
- **Student loan interest:** Federal student loan interest is tax-deductible
- **Bankruptcy:** Discharged after 9-21 months (first time), affects credit 6-7 years

## Credit Score Mechanics

### US: FICO Score (300-850)

| Factor | Weight | How to Optimize |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Payment history | 35% | Never miss a payment (set up auto-pay) |
| Credit utilization | 30% | Keep below 30%, ideally below 10% |
| Length of history | 15% | Don't close old cards |
| Credit mix | 10% | Mix of revolving + installment (don't open accounts just for this) |
| New inquiries | 10% | Limit new applications |

- **Free reports:** annualcreditreport.com (3 bureaus, free weekly)
- **Free scores:** Credit Karma, Discover, most bank apps

### Canada: Credit Score (300-900)
- Similar factors to US (payment history, utilization, length, mix, inquiries)
- Two bureaus: TransUnion and Equifax
- **Free reports:** Borrowell (Equifax), Credit Karma (TransUnion)
- Request free report directly from each bureau annually

### Quick Improvements
1. Set up auto-pay for all bills (payment history)
2. Pay credit card balance before statement date (lower reported utilization)
3. Request credit limit increases (lowers utilization ratio)
4. Become authorized user on family member's old card (length of history)
5. Dispute any errors on credit report

## Insurance Essentials

### US Insurance
- **Health:** Employer plan, ACA marketplace, or Medicaid. HSA-eligible high-deductible plans enable tax-advantaged savings.
- **Life:** Term life if you have dependents. Rule of thumb: 10-12x annual income. 20-30 year term. Avoid whole/universal life (expensive, poor returns).
- **Disability:** Often through employer. Replaces 60-70% of income if you can't work. Consider supplemental if employer coverage is insufficient.
- **Renters/Homeowners:** Renters ~$15-30/month, homeowners varies. Covers possessions and liability.
- **Auto:** Required in most states. Liability + comprehensive/collision. Increase deductible to lower premium.
- **Umbrella:** Extra liability coverage ($1M+ for $200-400/year). Worth it once you have assets to protect.

### Canada Insurance
- **Health:** Provincial plans cover basics (doctor visits, hospital). Supplemental covers dental, vision, prescriptions, paramedical (often through employer).
- **Life:** Same rule: 10-12x income, term policy, avoid permanent/whole life for most people.
- **Disability:** Critical for income protection. CPP disability benefit is modest. Group plans through employer or individual policies.
- **Critical illness:** Lump sum if diagnosed with covered condition. More common in Canada than US.
- **Home/Tenant:** Tenant insurance ~$20-40/month. Homeowners varies by province and property.
- **Auto:** Required in all provinces. Rates vary dramatically by province (highest in Ontario/BC).

## Behavioral Finance

### Automation is Key
- Automate savings transfers on payday
- Automate bill payments
- Automate investment contributions
- Remove decisions from the process (decision fatigue is real)

### Common Biases
- **Loss aversion:** Losses hurt 2x more than equivalent gains feel good. Leads to panic selling.
- **Anchoring:** Fixating on purchase price rather than current value/fundamentals.
- **Status quo bias:** Sticking with default even when change would help (e.g., staying in bad 401k fund).
- **Lifestyle inflation (hedonic adaptation):** Spending increases with income. Combat by automating savings increases with raises.
- **Present bias:** Overvaluing immediate rewards vs future benefits. Solution: make saving feel rewarding now.

### Money Mindset
- Your savings rate matters more than investment returns (especially early on)
- Wealth = what you don't spend (accumulation, not income)
- Compare yourself to your past self, not to others
- Financial literacy compounds like money does - small improvements add up
