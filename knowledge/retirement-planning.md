# Retirement Planning

Retirement strategies, government benefits, and FIRE methodology for US and Canada.

## Retirement Number Calculation

### The 4% Rule (Trinity Study)
- **Formula:** Annual expenses x 25 = retirement portfolio target
- Example: $50,000/year expenses -> need $1,250,000
- Based on: 4% withdrawal rate has historically survived 30-year periods with 95%+ success rate
- Assumes: 50/50 to 75/25 stock/bond portfolio

### Adjustments
- **Conservative (3.5%):** Expenses x ~29. For early retirees (40+ year horizon), lower expected returns
- **Moderate (4%):** Expenses x 25. Standard for traditional retirement at 60-65
- **Aggressive (4.5-5%):** Expenses x 20-22. If flexible spending, other income sources, shorter horizon
- **Variable withdrawal:** Adjust spending based on portfolio performance (guardrails strategy)

### Key Variables
- Social Security/CPP/OAS reduce the amount your portfolio needs to cover
- Part-time income in early retirement reduces withdrawal needs
- Healthcare costs (major factor in US, less so in Canada)
- Inflation: plan for expenses to increase ~2-3% annually

## US Retirement

### Social Security

**Eligibility:** 40 credits (approximately 10 years of work)

**Claiming Age Optimization:**

| Age | Benefit Level | Notes |
|-----|--------------|-------|
| 62 | ~70% of full | Permanent reduction, but more years of payments |
| 67 | 100% (full retirement age for born 1960+) | Standard benefit |
| 70 | ~124% of full | Maximum benefit, 8% increase per year past FRA |

**Break-even analysis:**
- 62 vs 67: break-even around age 78-80
- 67 vs 70: break-even around age 82-83
- If healthy and expect longevity: delay to 70
- If health concerns or need income now: claim earlier

**Spousal benefits:** Up to 50% of higher earner's full benefit
**Survivor benefits:** Up to 100% of deceased spouse's benefit
**Taxation:** Up to 85% of benefits taxable if income exceeds thresholds ($25K single / $32K married)

### Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)
- **Start age:** 73 (SECURE 2.0 Act)
- **Applies to:** Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA
- **Does NOT apply to:** Roth IRA (during owner's lifetime), Roth 401(k) (SECURE 2.0 removed this requirement)
- **Penalty for missing:** 25% of required amount (10% if corrected within 2 years)
- **Strategy:** Roth conversions before RMD age to reduce future required distributions

### Retirement Account Types

| Account | 2025 Limit | Tax Treatment | Withdrawal Rules |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| 401(k) / 403(b) | $23,500 (+$7,500 catch-up) | Pre-tax or Roth | 59.5+, RMDs at 73, Rule of 55 |
| Traditional IRA | $7,000 (+$1,000 catch-up) | Tax-deductible (income limits apply) | 59.5+, RMDs at 73, 10% penalty before |
| Roth IRA | $7,000 (+$1,000 catch-up) | After-tax, tax-free growth | Contributions anytime, gains at 59.5 + 5yr rule |
| SEP IRA | $69,000 | Pre-tax | 59.5+, RMDs at 73 |
| Solo 401(k) | $69,000 total | Pre-tax or Roth | Same as 401(k) |

### Early Retirement / FIRE Strategies

**Roth Conversion Ladder**
1. Convert Traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth IRA each year in early retirement
2. Each conversion has a 5-year waiting period before penalty-free withdrawal
3. Fill lower tax brackets with conversions (e.g., convert $50K/year at 12% bracket)
4. After 5 years: withdraw converted amounts tax-free and penalty-free
5. Bridge years 1-5 with taxable account, Roth contributions, or other income

**72(t) / SEPP (Substantially Equal Periodic Payments)**
- Withdraw from IRA before 59.5 without 10% penalty
- Must take substantially equal payments for 5 years or until 59.5 (whichever is later)
- Calculation methods: required minimum distribution, fixed amortization, fixed annuitization
- Inflexible: changing the amount triggers retroactive penalties
- Best for: people with large IRA balances who need income before 59.5

**Rule of 55**
- Access 401(k) penalty-free if separated from employer at age 55 or later
- Only applies to the 401(k) at the employer you left at 55+
- Strategy: roll old 401(k)s into current employer's plan before leaving

**FIRE Bridge Strategy**
1. Years 1-5: Live off taxable brokerage + Roth contributions
2. Years 1-5 simultaneously: Roth conversion ladder (fill low tax brackets)
3. Year 5+: Roth conversion ladder funds become available
4. Year 59.5+: Full access to all retirement accounts
5. Year 62+: Social Security available (consider delaying to 67-70)

## Canada Retirement

### CPP (Canada Pension Plan)

**Eligibility:** At least 1 valid contribution

**Claiming Optimization:**

| Age | Adjustment | Monthly Max (2025 est.) |
|-----|-----------|------------------------|
| 60 | -36% (0.6%/month x 60 months) | ~$873 |
| 65 | Standard | ~$1,364 |
| 70 | +42% (0.7%/month x 60 months) | ~$1,937 |

**Break-even:** ~Age 74 for 60 vs 65, ~age 82 for 65 vs 70
**CPP2 enhancement:** Additional contributions (2024+) provide higher benefits for those earning above first ceiling
**Pension sharing:** Split CPP with spouse to reduce combined tax (both must be 60+)

### OAS (Old Age Security)
- **Universal:** Available at 65 with 10+ years of Canadian residency (40 years for full amount)
- **Maximum monthly (2025):** ~$727
- **GIS (Guaranteed Income Supplement):** Additional benefit for low-income seniors
- **OAS clawback:** Recovery tax starts at ~$90,997 income (2025), fully clawed back at ~$148K
- **Deferral:** Can defer to age 70 for 36% increase (0.6%/month)
- **Strategy:** If income will be high at 65, consider deferring. If income will be low, take at 65 and get GIS.

### RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund)
- Must convert RRSP to RRIF by December 31 of year turning 71
- Minimum withdrawal schedule increases with age:

| Age | Minimum Withdrawal % |
|-----|---------------------|
| 71 | 5.28% |
| 75 | 5.82% |
| 80 | 6.82% |
| 85 | 8.51% |
| 90 | 11.92% |

- All withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
- **Pension income splitting:** At 65+, can split up to 50% of RRIF income with spouse (reduces combined tax)
- **Pension income tax credit:** First $2,000 of eligible pension income qualifies for federal and provincial tax credits
- **RRSP meltdown strategy:** Draw down RRSP before 71 in low-income years to reduce future RRIF minimums and OAS clawback

### Canadian FIRE Considerations
- **TFSA advantage:** No penalty for early withdrawal at any age, tax-free
- **RRSP early withdrawal:** Withholding tax applies: 10% (up to $5K), 20% ($5K-$15K), 30% (>$15K). Added to taxable income for the year.
- **Non-registered accounts:** Capital gains (50% inclusion) and eligible dividends (tax credit) provide tax-efficient bridge income
- **Healthcare:** Provincial health plans cover basics at any age (major advantage over US FIRE)
- **No Roth conversion ladder equivalent:** But TFSA provides similar tax-free withdrawal flexibility

**Canadian FIRE Bridge Strategy**
1. Build TFSA to maximum (tax-free withdrawals anytime)
2. Non-registered portfolio for additional bridge income (capital gains + dividends)
3. RRSP meltdown: withdraw in low-income years before 71 to minimize tax
4. CPP at 60-65 depending on portfolio size and income needs
5. OAS at 65 (unless high income triggers clawback, then defer)

## General Retirement Principles

### Sequence-of-Returns Risk
- A major market decline early in retirement is devastating (selling at low prices depletes portfolio)
- **Mitigation:** Keep 1-2 years of expenses in cash/bonds, reduce equity allocation at retirement, variable withdrawal strategy
- **Bucket strategy:** Bucket 1 (1-2 years cash), Bucket 2 (3-7 years bonds), Bucket 3 (8+ years equities)

### Inflation Protection
- **US:** TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities), I-Bonds ($10K/year limit)
- **Canada:** Real-return bonds (limited supply), inflation-indexed annuities
- **Both:** Equities provide long-term inflation hedge (companies raise prices)

### Part-Time Work / Barista FIRE
- Semi-retirement with some earned income
- Reduces portfolio withdrawal needs significantly
- In US: can provide health insurance (major benefit)
- Psychologically easier transition than full stop
